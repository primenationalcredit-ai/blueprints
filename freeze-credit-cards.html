üéØ Why This Works

If your credit card is in your wallet, it's TOO EASY to use. One swipe and you've spent money you don't have. By making your cards physically inconvenient to access, you create a "cooling off" period that stops impulse purchases.

üß† The Psychology

Research shows that adding ANY friction to a purchase reduces impulse buying by up to 70%. If you have to go home, thaw a card, or ask someone for it - you'll think twice!

üßä Choose Your Method

Pick the method that works best for your situation:

‚ùÑÔ∏è Method 1: The Ice Block (Classic)

How: Put your credit card(s) in a container of water and freeze it.

  • Takes 2-3 hours to thaw = plenty of time to reconsider
  • Microwaving will damage the chip - you HAVE to wait
  • Visual reminder every time you open the freezer

Best for: People who need a serious barrier

üîí Method 2: The Lockbox

How: Put cards in a lockbox or safe. Give the key to someone you trust.

  • You have to ask someone to use your card
  • The embarrassment of asking stops most purchases
  • Good if you have a supportive partner/friend

Best for: People with accountability partners

üì¶ Method 3: The Hidden Drawer

How: Put cards in a drawer at home, not in your wallet.

  • Can't spend what you don't have with you
  • Online purchases still possible (remove saved cards too!)
  • Good first step if freezing feels too extreme

Best for: Starting out with card control

üë• Method 4: Give to Someone

How: Physically give your card to a trusted person.

  • They become your "approval system"
  • You have to explain why you need it
  • Great for couples working on finances together

Best for: Couples or close accountability relationships
