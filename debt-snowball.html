üéØ What You'll Achieve

The debt snowball method attacks your smallest balance first, regardless of interest rate. You get quick wins that build confidence and momentum. Each paid-off debt fuels your motivation to crush the next one. It's the method that actually works for most people.

Quick Wins

üìä How Snowball Works

The Simple 4-Step Process:

  1. List all debts by balance (smallest to largest)
  2. Pay minimums on everything except the smallest balance
  3. Throw ALL extra money at the smallest debt
  4. When that's paid off, roll that payment to the next smallest‚Äîthe snowball grows!

üß† Why It Works (The Science)

Research shows that people who use the snowball method are more likely to eliminate ALL their debt. Why? Quick wins release dopamine, creating positive reinforcement. Each paid-off debt proves you CAN do this, making you more likely to continue. The math might favor avalanche, but human psychology favors snowball.

‚öñÔ∏è Snowball vs Avalanche

‚õÑ Snowball (Smallest Balance First)

  • Quick early wins
  • Builds confidence & momentum
  • Higher completion rate
  • Pays slightly more interest
  • Best if you need motivation

üèîÔ∏è Avalanche (Highest Rate First)

  • Mathematically optimal
  • Pays least total interest
  • Big debts may take longer
  • Requires more patience
  • Best if motivated by savings

üí° The Truth About Both Methods

The BEST debt payoff method is the one you'll actually stick with. Avalanche saves more money mathematically, but snowball has higher success rates because quick wins keep people motivated. Choose based on your personality, not just the math.

üí° Snowball Power-Ups

  • Sell stuff: Facebook Marketplace, eBay‚Äîturn clutter into debt payments
  • Side hustle: Even $200/month extra crushes debt faster
  • Tax refund: Throw the whole thing at the snowball
  • Visualize: Put a chart on the fridge, color it in as you pay down
  • Tell someone: Accountability makes you 65% more likely to succeed

‚ö†Ô∏è Don't Sabotage Your Snowball

  • Don't add new debt: Cut up the cards, stop the bleeding
  • Keep a small emergency fund: $1,000 prevents derailing for emergencies
  • Don't skip minimums: Late fees destroy your progress
  • Don't reward with spending: Celebrate wins, but don't spend your way backward
