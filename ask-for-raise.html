üìã Your Action Plan

Step 1: Research Your Market Value

Find out what people in your role actually make. Use these free tools:

  • Glassdoor.com - Search your job title + city
  • Salary.com - Detailed salary ranges
  • LinkedIn Salary - Real data from your network
  • PayScale.com - Personalized salary report
üí° Pro Tip: Write down the range you find. If you're below the midpoint, you have a strong case!

Step 2: Document Your Wins

List 3-5 specific accomplishments. Use numbers whenever possible!

üéØ Projects Completed

"Led the X project that..."

üí∞ Money Saved/Earned

"Reduced costs by $X..."

üìà Metrics Improved

"Increased sales/efficiency by X%..."

üë• Extra Responsibilities

"Took on training new hires..."

‚úÖ Good Example:

"I streamlined our inventory process, which reduced ordering errors by 40% and saved the company approximately $15,000 this year."

Step 3: Pick Your Number

Decide on a specific amount or percentage to ask for. Be realistic but don't lowball yourself!

üí° The 10-15% Rule: For strong performers, asking for 10-15% is reasonable. If you're significantly underpaid, you might ask for more with market data to back it up.

Step 4: Request a Meeting

Don't ambush your boss. Schedule a dedicated time to talk.

üìß Email to Send:

"Hi [Boss], I'd like to schedule a meeting to discuss my role and compensation. Would you have 20-30 minutes this week? I'm flexible on timing."

Step 5: Have THE Conversation

üìû The Script:

"Thank you for meeting with me. I really enjoy working here and I'm committed to the team's success. Over the past [time period], I've [accomplishment 1], [accomplishment 2], and [accomplishment 3].

Based on my contributions and market research showing that similar roles pay [range], I'd like to discuss increasing my salary to [target amount]. What are your thoughts?"

‚ö†Ô∏è Important: Then STOP TALKING. Let them respond. Silence is powerful‚Äîdon't fill it with nervous chatter or immediately lower your ask.

Step 6: Handle Their Response

If they say "Yes":

"Thank you! I really appreciate it. Can you send me the details in writing so I can review them?"

If they say "Not now" or "Let me think about it":

"I understand. Can we set a specific date to revisit this conversation? And what would you need to see from me to make this happen?"

If they say "No":

"I appreciate your honesty. Can you help me understand what it would take to get to [target amount]? Are there other forms of compensation we could discuss, like extra PTO, a bonus, or flexible work arrangements?"

Step 7: Get It In Writing

If you get a raise, make sure it's documented! Ask for an updated offer letter or email confirmation.

üí™ If You Don't Get the Raise

It's not the end! You've planted a seed. Here's what to do:

  • Ask what it would take - Get specific milestones
  • Set a follow-up date - "Can we revisit in 3 months?"
  • Consider other options - Bonus, extra PTO, remote work
  • Start looking elsewhere - You might find better pay externally
üí° Fun Fact: External hires often get 10-20% more than internal promotions. Sometimes the best raise comes from a new job!
